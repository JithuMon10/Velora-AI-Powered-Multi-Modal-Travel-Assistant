ABSTRACT


India’s public transport ecosystem is inherently complex, spanning long‑distance trains, interstate and intrastate buses, metros, flights and a rich layer of informal last‑mile connectivity. Commuters are often forced to manually piece together information from multiple sources such as railway time‑tables, bus operator web‑sites, ticketing portals, mapping applications and word‑of‑mouth knowledge. This leads to sub‑optimal route choices, longer travel times and higher costs, especially for long distance or multi‑city journeys. Existing journey planning applications typically focus on a single mode of transport, single city coverage or static schedules, and rarely combine AI‑assisted decision support with real‑time routing engines.


The micro project VELORA – AI‑POWERED MULTIMODAL TRANSIT PLANNER FOR INDIA aims to address this gap by designing and implementing a prototype system that helps a user plan a complete journey across India using a combination of buses, trains, flights and driving. The system integrates a PHP‑based backend, a MySQL database of stops and services, and an interactive web frontend built using HTML, CSS, JavaScript and the Leaflet mapping library. For routing, the system leverages the open‑source GraphHopper engine and OpenStreetMap (OSM) data to compute realistic road‑based itineraries when schedule data is unavailable, and uses a custom trip‑planning algorithm on top of stored bus and train schedules when structured timetable data exists.


Velora offers an AI‑assisted interface that accepts origin and destination as natural text, supports auto‑complete for known stops, and progressively reveals advanced options such as mode filters, arrival‑by time, hotel suggestions near the destination and comparative route summaries. The frontend provides a rich, Google‑Maps‑like user experience with floating search cards, an interactive map, route overlays, step‑by‑step instructions and optional voice guidance using the Web Speech API. The backend exposes REST‑style endpoints for fetching stops, buses, trains and hotels, and for planning a trip that returns a JSON representation of legs, fares and timings. A modular helper layer encapsulates graph routing, realistic travel time estimation, fare calculation and integration with external APIs such as GraphHopper.


The project demonstrates how open‑source routing engines, public datasets and a carefully designed web application can be combined to deliver a practical and scalable transit planning solution tailored to the Indian context. The report presents the motivation and problem statement, the detailed system requirements, database and software design, algorithmic approach, implementation details and representative test results. It also analyses limitations of the current prototype and outlines future enhancements such as full GTFS integration, dynamic traffic‑aware routing, multilingual interfaces and mobile‑first deployments.

CHAPTER 1 INTRODUCTION

1.1 Background

India is the most populous democracy in the world, with a vast land area and a vibrant, constantly moving population. Cities like Mumbai, Delhi, Bengaluru, and Chennai face mobility bottlenecks daily, while rural regions are often underserved. Indian commuters balance cost, reliability, congestion, multimodal transfers, and information uncertainty at every step of their journey. The complexity is compounded by fragmented operators, informal transit, and the lack of a consistent, all-India digital platform for seamless route planning across bus, train, metro, flight, and taxi.

Traditional navigation tools offer limited multimodal support and rarely cater to India's unique constraints—overnight travel, unpredictable schedules, regional languages, complex fares, and disconnected ticketing. The need for AI-powered, location intelligence-driven, user-friendly transit planners is acute, especially for long-distance journeys, cross-state travel, and 'last mile' connection in urban fringes and villages.

1.2 Motivation

India’s fast growing urbanization, combined with the challenges of mobility for rural and less-connected populations, inspired the development of the Velora project. After hearing stories of students missing buses due to wrong apps, families struggling to estimate trip costs, and tourists bewildered by conflicting maps, the need for a smart, India-tailored transit planner became undeniable. The vision: anyone, anywhere in India, can enter a start/end and receive real, actionable options—regardless of city, region, or language.

Further motivation came from the rise of digital mapping, advances in AI, and the success of multimodal planners in major Western countries. But no open, student-built solution existed for India's massive, dynamic landscape using open-source data, LAMP stack, and modern web user experience.

1.3 The Indian Public Transport Landscape

Indian transport is an intricate ecosystem: over 70,000 trains daily, multiple bus corporations (state and private), fledgling metro systems, ubiquitous autos, cycle-rickshaws, ferries, ride apps, and millions of intercity cabs. Mobility patterns change by region, festival, and season. Digital schedule data is patchy; booking portals are siloed. In many villages, directions depend on word-of-mouth or handwritten wall schedules. Students travel hundreds of kilometers for entrance exams; rural patients need multi-leg hospital trips; office workers combine trains, feeder buses, and shared autos.

Our literature review found most planners serve large metros but ignore these realities: the lack of last-mile data, unreliable scheduling, non-standardized 'hops' (e.g., 'walk 2km to bus stand'), and variable connection times. Even where apps/portals exist, they duplicate effort and are not integrated for price or time optimization across modes and operators.

1.4 Gap Analysis

Most existing web planners and transit apps for India are either restricted to one city, one operator, or one transport mode. International apps like Google Maps and Rome2Rio, though powerful, often lack coverage for India's trains, private buses, or feeder and informal services, with minimal schedule accuracy during holidays or monsoon disruptions. State RTC portals only show their own buses, ignoring train/bus combos or providing only basic lookup without door-to-door planning.

Academic prototypes from India, if published, typically lack public deployments or contribute only timetable research, not real-world route-finding tools for the masses.

1.5 Aim & Objectives

  • To design and implement an AI-powered, user-centric web-based transit planner for the Indian context.
  • To enable seamless journey planning across bus, train, and other modes using both real-time API data and stored schedules.
  • To support fare estimation, hotel search near destination, support for regional languages, and integration of weather and travel advisories.
  • To provide a responsive UI, mobile/desktop support, and ‘step by step’ route guidance including alternate/backup routes and last mile.
  • To demonstrate the use of the LAMP stack (Linux, Apache, MySQL, PHP) for rapid, scalable web prototyping in an Indian microproject environment.
  • To propose a scalable design that could be extended to full GTFS and national coverage.

1.6 Scope

This micro project implements a working prototype, not a commercial product. The current system demonstrates core transit planning, schedule dbs, mapping, UI, and summary comparison (ex: time, cost, wait) for several sample intercity and local trips. Tuning for load, full India schedule integration, or real-world mobile deployment is future work. However, the modular codebase, separation of UI/backend/routing, and interface-first design allow easy extension to new sources and data.
